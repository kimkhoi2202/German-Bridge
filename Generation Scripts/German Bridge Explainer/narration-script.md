# German Bridge Explainer Narration

Source narration used for the ElevenLabs voiceover.

```text
German Bridge is a trick-taking card game where the goal is simple: predict exactly how many tricks you will win.

At the start of a hand, choose the number of players, decks, and tricks per hand. Then the cards are dealt, and one extra card is flipped face up. Its suit becomes trump.

Now everyone bids. Your bid is the number of tricks you think you can take this hand. You can bid zero, one, two, all the way up to the number of tricks available.

There is one important twist: the last bidder is not allowed to make the total number of bids equal the total number of tricks. That means someone at the table must be wrong, which keeps every hand competitive.

Once bidding is done, play begins. The first player leads a card. Everyone else must follow the lead suit if they can. If they cannot follow suit, they may play any card.

The highest card in the lead suit wins, unless a trump card is played. Trump beats the lead suit. If multiple identical cards appear because you are playing with more than one deck, the later-played identical card wins.

Each trick winner leads the next trick. Keep going until every card has been played.

Scoring is what makes German Bridge sharp. If you make your bid exactly, you score ten plus the square of the tricks you won. So bidding two and winning two scores fourteen.

If you miss your bid, you lose the square of the difference. Bid three and win one? That is minus four.

So the whole game is about reading your hand, making the right bid, and then playing carefully enough to hit it.

That is German Bridge: bid exact, follow suit, respect trump, and try to take exactly what you promised.
```
